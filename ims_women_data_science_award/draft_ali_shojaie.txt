Old letter from Ali (thank you!):

I am pleased to provide this letter in support of Dr. Jean Feng’s promotion to Associate Professor
at UCSF.
Before presenting my assessment of Dr. Feng’s accomplishments, I will first discuss the extent of
my prior interactions and familiarity with her work.
Background: I am a Professor of Biostatistics and Statistics at the University of Washington (UW).
I am also the Associate Chair for Research at the Department of Biostatistics. I have had the pleasure
of knowing Dr. Feng since she joined the Department of Biostatistics at UW in 2015. During her
time at UW, Jean was a star student and worked with my colleague and collaborator, Dr. Noah
Simon (as well as Dr. Erick Matsen). My first scientific interactions with Jean were after she joined
the working group that Dr. Simon and I run at UW Biostatistics. From the beginning, it was clear
that Jean was an amazing scholar and soon became a leader among the students/trainees in the lab.
I also had the pleasure of serving as a member of Jean’s dissertation committee and am thus quite
familiar with her PhD research. Despite extensive interactions and familiarity with Jean’s work, my
only collaboration with her has been via teaching: Jean and I co-taught a short course on machine
learning and artificial intelligence (ML/AI) at the 6th Seattle Symposium in Biostatistics: Precision
Health in the Age of Data Science. More recently, Jean and I extended this course and co-taught a
2.5-day short course on Supervised Machine Learning for Biomedical Big Data at the Summer
Institute in Statistics for Big Data (SISBID), which I direct.
I will next provide my assessment in each of the requested criteria.
Research accomplishments and promise: Even while working on her dissertation, it was clear
that Jean was an outstanding researcher with broad interests, as she worked on several important
and challenging problems in diverse scientific areas. Jean was also prolific, completing ten projects
in less than four years! Jean’s ability to tackle diverse problems is to a large part due to her
outstanding skills in various aspects of statistical machine learning – from modeling and
computation to theory. However, an equally important factor is Jean’s ability to identify important
and emerging biomedical questions.
I have followed Jean’s work after she left UW, both because I strongly believe she is a rising star
in the field of Biostatistics, and also because her work often develops elegant, yet practical solutions
for interesting scientific problems. Jean’s most recent work on interpretability and reliability of
machine learning methods for biomedical applications demonstrates her growth as a scholar. In this
work, she has made important contributions to characterizing uncertainty of black-box ML/AI
models and their safe/ethical use in biomedical studies. This is an important and emerging area of
research that will provide Jean with great opportunities to apply her diverse skills to not only make
important scientific contributions, but to also improve public health and medicine as ML/Al become
mainstream in the field
Jean's scholarly publications are impressive in quantity and quality and have already had a
considerable impact on the field. Jean has also been successful in securing grant funding for her
research. In addition to an (internal) UCSF grant, she has also obtained a PCORI grant along with
a supplement that funds her current research.
Excellence in teaching/mentoring: Jean is a great communicator and an excellent teacher. During
her PhD, she gave many clear and inspiring presentations at our join lab meetings. She has also
given multiple invited presentations at national and international conferences, including a talk at an
invited session that I had organized for the 2023 Joint Statistical Meetings (JSM). As mentioned
above, I have also witnessed Jean's effectiveness in teaching in a short course that we co-taught on
ML/AI. Jean's recognition as a leading scholar in this field and her teaching excellence are likely
key reasons for her invitation to teach a Machine Learning Boot Camp at Columbia University.
Jean's current advising - one PhD student, one postdoc and one junior faculty (on K23 grant) - as
well as her outreach activities (through mentoring high school students) make a clear case for her
passion and skills for mentoring students and junior researchers.
Local stature in the field: Jean is widely recognition within the Biostatistics community as evident
from the impressive number of invited talks and seminars she has delivered, including talks at top
Biostatistics departments. Jean's work has also been recognized by her recent funding from the
PCORI program, indicating that a panel of reviewers deem her work innovative and significant.
Service to the profession: Jean has provided service and leadership at various capacities, from
organizing and chairing scientific sessions at multiple conferences to extensive referee services.
She has also served on several committees at UCSF. These include committees focused on ML/AI
as well as committees in the Department. More recently, Jean has been selected as one of the
statistical reviewers for NEJM-AI, which further underscores the recognition of her work by the
broader community. Overall, Jean seems to have provided extensive service to the University and
community.
Summary: In summary, I believe, Dr. Feng has had impressive achievements in research, teaching
and is among the rising stars in the field of Biostatistics. I look forward to following her continued
success and her growing impact in the field. Given these accomplishments, I believe Jean clearly
meets the criteria for promotion to Associate Professor.
